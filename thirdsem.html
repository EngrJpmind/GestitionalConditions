PLACENTA PREVIA

Pepito, Baby Jean B.
Codera , Sharina Marie

Introduction

The placenta has implanted at the bottom of the uterus, over the cervix or close by, which means the baby can't be born vaginally. Treatment aims to ease the symptoms and prolong the pregnancy until at least 36 weeks. If you experience any bleeding during pregnancy, it is important to contact your healthcare provider immediately. They will be able to evaluate your symptoms and determine the best course of action. In the case of GTD, early diagnosis and treatment are important for a successful outcome.

Most common month/trimester to experience bleeding

The most common symptom of placenta previa is bright red, painless bleeding from the vagina. This is most common in the third trimester of pregnancy.

Cause

The exact cause of placenta previa is unknown.

Risk factors

Placenta previa is more common among women who:


• Have had a baby
• Have had a previous C-section delivery
• Have scars on the uterus from a previous surgery or procedure
• Had placenta previa with a previous pregnancy
• Are pregnant after having an assisted reproductive technology (ART) procedure for treating infertility
• Are carrying more than one fetus
• Are age 35 or older
• Smoke
• Use cocaine

Complications

If you have placenta previa, your health care provider will monitor you and your baby to reduce the risk of these serious complications:


• Bleeding. Severe, possibly life-threatening vaginal bleeding (hemorrhage) can occur during the pregnancy, labor, delivery or in the first few hours after delivery.
• Preterm birth. Severe bleeding may prompt an emergency C-section before your baby is full term.
• Placenta accreta spectrum. Placenta previa is associated with a group of conditions called placenta accreta spectrum. With these conditions, the placenta grows into or through the wall of the uterus. Placenta accreta has a high risk of bleeding during pregnancy or during and after delivery.

ABRUPTIO PLACENTA

Labrador, Jun Steiner
Loberanes , Kathlyn Kaye

Symptoms

Symptoms of abruptio placenta can include vaginal bleeding, abdominal pain, uterine contractions, and fetal distress. In some cases, there may be no visible bleeding, but the mother and baby may still experience symptoms.

Risk factors

Risk factors for abruptio placenta include high blood pressure, smoking, cocaine use, trauma to the abdomen, and previous episodes of placental abruption.

Diagnosis

A healthcare provider will typically diagnose abruptio placenta based on symptoms, physical examination, and imaging tests such as ultrasound.

Treatment

Treatment for abruptio placenta depends on the severity of the condition and the stage of pregnancy. In some cases, bed rest and close monitoring may be sufficient. In more severe cases, delivery of the baby and placenta may be necessary, even if the baby is premature.

Complications

Abruptio placenta can lead to serious complications for both the mother and baby, including hemorrhage, shock, stillbirth, and maternal death.

Prevention

There are some steps that women can take to reduce their risk of abruptio placenta, such as managing high blood pressure, avoiding smoking and drug use, and seeking prompt medical attention if they experience trauma to the abdomen.

Prognosis

The prognosis for abruptio placenta depends on the severity of the condition and the gestational age of the baby. With prompt diagnosis and treatment, many women and babies are able to recover fully. However, severe cases can have serious and long-lasting effects.

Incidence

According to a study published in the Philippine Journal of Obstetrics and Gynecology, the incidence of abruptio placenta in the Philippines is approximately 0.6% (Hernandez et al., 2016).

Risk factors

Risk factors for abruptio placenta include maternal age over 35 years old, multiple pregnancies, hypertension, smoking, and previous history of placental abruption.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis of abruptio placenta is typically made based on clinical symptoms such as vaginal bleeding and abdominal pain, as well as diagnostic tests such as ultrasound.

Treatment

Treatment for abruptio placenta typically involves delivery of the baby and placenta. In some cases, conservative management may be attempted if the fetus is not yet viable.

Complications

Complications of abruptio placenta can include maternal hemorrhage, preterm birth, and fetal distress

Prevention

Prevention strategies for abruptio placenta include prenatal care to identify and manage risk factors, avoidance of smoking and drug use during pregnancy, and timely delivery of the baby in cases of severe or life-threatening placental abruption

Prognosis

The prognosis for abruptio placenta depends on the severity of the condition and the gestational age of the fetus. With prompt diagnosis and treatment, many women and babies are able to recover fully. However, severe cases can have serious and long-lasting effects
